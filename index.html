Curriculum Vitae

I am an ETH Zurich Postdoctoral Fellow in the International Relations and Data Science Lab. I received my PhD in Political Science from the University of California, Berkeley, and hold an MPA/ID from the Harvard Kennedy School. I am also a Research Associate at the Center on the Politics of Development.

I am a political scientist studying how social hierarchies—especially gender, race, and immigration status—shape how individuals engage institutions under conditions of inequality and violence. My research spans the United States and India, with projects on immigration, racial attitudes, and gender-based violence. I focus in particular on administrative and archival data and building person-level datasets from institutional records to understand how policies translate into lived outcomes.

Methodologically, I combine fieldwork with surveys, experiments, and LLMs. I use primary survey and archival data as well as large-scale administrative data.

Working Papers

How She Leaves: Women's Organizations, Violence, and Strategic Reporting in India

Abstract

Why do survivors of domestic violence rarely engage state institutions, even as governments expand formal remedies? I argue that institutional demand depends on what communities believe institutions will deliver—not only on their formal capacity. Using a vignette experiment with 2,031 married women in rural Gujarat, I show that respondents hold differentiated beliefs about institutional function. Women’s organizations are perceived as significantly more likely to reduce violence and support residential separation than caste organizations or women’s police, while no institution is seen as distinctively better at coercion or facilitating compromise. These beliefs are widely shared even among respondents with no direct exposure to these institutions, indicating that they reflect categorical understandings rather than organization-specific experience. Drawing on an original archive of case files, I show that these beliefs are well-calibrated: women’s organizations operate as intermediaries that navigate across caste bodies, police, and courts to enable separation without divorce—the most feasible form of exit under constrained conditions. By centering community expectations, the paper offers a demand-side theory of institutional engagement in legally plural settings and shows that rights implementation depends on whether institutions make exit from coercive relationships credible.

Missing and Miscoded Women: A Note on Measuring Domestic Violence Claims

Abstract

Why do many cases of domestic violence never appear in official data? Existing accounts emphasize barriers to reporting or institutional failure. Using the case of rural Gujarat, I offer an alternative explanation: observed reporting patterns can also reflect strategic institutional choice under constraint. Drawing on an original archive of 368 case files from a local women’s organization, and a pilot analysis of a randomly sampled subset, I develop a coding framework to trace how survivors engage institutions and how these pathways shape measurement. I show that survivors’ relational objectives generate two forms of measurement error: cases that are missing from official domestic violence data and cases that are miscoded under alternative legal categories. Rather than seeking punishment, survivors use institutions to negotiate, exert pressure, or secure material support within ongoing marital relationships. These pathways systematically shape how cases are recorded across institutional settings. Using administrative data alone therefore risks conflating barriers to access with women’s strategic engagement with institutions, obscuring how agency is exercised through relational objectives under constraint.
